PAKISTAN
S
TABILITY BECOMES ADVANTAGE
Pakistan's Week 16 extends and arguably intensifies the stability theme that defined Week 15. The
Sta bility holds. Pakistani Rupee has held in a narrow range near 278.90 through the week, essentially unchanged
from the close of Week 14 and Week 15, and local steel plate prices at PKR 188,000 per ton have
pushed further above the Week 14 level, translating to a USD equivalent near USD 671 per ton.
This places Gadani at its firmest absolute USD pricing position of the year. The gap to India has
widened modestly to approximately USD 230 per ton at current levels, reaffirming Pakistan's
position as the sub-continent's second-most competitive destination with accelerating momentum.
March 2026 CPI at 7.3% sits above the State Bank's 5% to 7% target, driven primarily by transport
at plus 12.5% on the month and housing, but the trajectory remains manageable for yard economics
given the stable PKR base.
The continued closure of the Strait of Hormuz and the US naval blockade of Iranian ports are, on
Ad vantage builds. balance, reinforcing Gadani's structural positioning rather than eroding it. The speculative risk
noted in Week 15, that a broader de-escalation might moderate Pakistan's Gulf proximity
advantage, has receded with the narrowing of the US-Iran talks from full settlement to a temporary
memorandum. Three HKC-certified yards are now operational with further certifications in the
pipeline, and the pool of recycling candidates consisting of medium-sized dry bulk, LPG, and
offshore units continues to align well with Gadani's available capacity. The compliance profile is
improving. The pricing position is firming. The Gulf logistics advantage is holding. All three
reinforcements are operating simultaneously, which is unusual.
The one remaining unknown for Pakistan is whether the structural position translates into
Timing risk. executed transactions before the regional dynamic shifts. A further extension of the ceasefire, or
any development suggesting a durable resolution of the Hormuz situation, would begin to erode
the proximity premium that has been directing tonnage toward Gadani. For now, this remains a
speculative risk rather than an immediate market reality. Pakistan enters the second half of Q2's
pre-monsoon window on the firmest footing of its year, with predictable cost inputs, expanding
compliance credentials, and a competitive pricing position that is strengthening rather than
weakening. The supply variable is shared equally with Bangladesh and India. Whether the oil
pullback becomes the catalyst that finally releases the Q1 backlog will be the defining question of
the coming weeks, and in that question, Pakistan has perhaps the most to lose if the answer comes
quickly, and the most to consolidate if it does not.
