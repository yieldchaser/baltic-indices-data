1. Introduction
Ocean transportation is the cornerstone of globalization and the main contributor of international
transportation networks that enable international trade and underpin global supply chains. As one
of the most important elements of the ocean shipping market, shipping freight rates play an
important role in regulating the supply and demand of sea transport, and directly affect revenues
and profits of ocean carriers.
The term ‘freight rate' is widely used in ocean shipping studies. It can be generally defined as the
‘amount of money paid to a shipowner or shipping line for the carriage of each unit of cargo (tonnage,
cubic meter, or container load) between named ports' (Stopford 2009). However, it often means
different subjects across the ocean shipping sectors and contract types. The data and methods
applied in the literature also reflect the different fundamental understandings of the definition of
‘freight rate' in ocean shipping. Notably, for the bulk shipping sectors (i.e., dry bulk and tanker), the
freight rate is essentially the vessel hiring price (formally called as vessel fixture rate), which can be
a time-limited hiring (a time charter [TC] contract) or a mission-limited hiring (a voyage charter
contract). In contrast, the container shipping sector has a clear two-level market structure, that is,

shipowners, and the ‘lower' freight market where the shipment price is paid by the shippers to the

Agreements (FFAs) (traded either as freight futures or freight options) are used (Stopford 2009),
although strictly, these are not freight rates.
Accurate explanation and forecasting of the shipping freight rates have significant implications
for practitioners, economists, and regulators. The annual standard deviation of the Baltic Capesize
Index (BCI) was almost 40% from March 1999 to October 2011, more than twice as high as the
volatility of the Morgan Stanley Capital International (MSCI) World Stock Market Index (Drobetz,
Richter, and Wambach 2012). Stopford (2009) identified 22 dry cargo freight cycles between 1741
and 2007. The volatile and cyclical shipping freight rates bring great challenges to shipowners,
charterers, and shippers to successfully plan their business, and to shipping investors to hedge and
speculate based on freight rate changes.
The overarching goal of the research on shipping freight rates should be to improve the under-
standing of the dynamic behaviours of freight rates, as well as their determination. However, due to
the lack of comprehensive reviews, it remains unclear how much improvement has been made
towards this goal, as well as challenges and gaps. There have been literature reviews of shipping
market research. Alexandridis et al. (2018) summarized papers on freight rate volatility and
volatility spillovers across shipping segments when conducting an extensive review on shipping
finance research. Two reviews specifically on shipping freight rates were published in 2006, by Glen
(2006) and Kavussanos and Visvikis (2006), focusing on model development in the bulk shipping
market and the shipping derivatives market, respectively. As the number of papers published has
increased greatly, especially after 2010, an updated review is highly needed.
For this purpose, this paper systematically reviews the academic articles on quantitative models
of shipping freight rates published between 2000 and 2019 across all three shipping sectors with
four general research objectives. Following the guidance of Wee, Bert and Banister (2016) for review
studies, this paper aims to provide added values to the literature by (i) providing empirical insights
on shipping freight rates, including what factors have been proven to have an impact on shipping
freight rates, what are the major differences of freight rates among different shipping sectors; (ii)
analysing the dominant methods and their applicability, strengths and weaknesses, and opportu-
nities for new methods; and (iii) exploring the omissions and limitations in existing literature to
define the gaps in the literature and set a future research agenda.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: the second section introduces the review
methodology and an overview of the studies. In the third section, the reviewed papers are
categorized into four research objective groups, followed by a discussion of the quantitative models
under each group. The fourth and fifth sections thoroughly discuss main findings and challenges
regarding freight rate influencing factors and time series characteristics, respectively. Additionally,
the sixth section provides an analysis of collaboration patterns in this area. The last section presents
the conclusions of this paper.
2. Research method
The relevant studies are searched and selected through an iterative process of defining appropriate
searching terms, reviewing the literature, conducting initial analysis, and finalizing classification
results. The details of the search methodology are as follows:
2.1. Search methodology
A computerized search is used to collect journal publications on shipping freight rates. The database
Web of Science is first searched with the keywords ‘freight rate', ‘freight market', ‘spot rate', ‘charter
rate,' ‘Baltic Dry Index (BDI)', ‘Baltic Dirty Tanker Index (BDTI)', ‘Baltic Clean Tanker Index

(BCTI)', ‘Shanghai Containerized Freight Index (SCFI)', and ‘China (Export) Containerized Freight
Index (CCFI)' combined with ‘shipping', ‘maritime', ‘dry bulk', ‘container', and ‘tanker'.1 The same
search is then expanded to different publishers' websites, including Elsevier (www.sciencedirect.

2.2. Inclusion/exclusion criteria
This review considers peer-reviewed academic articles studying shipping freight rates that were
written in English over the last 20 years, from 2000 to 2019. Other forms of publications, such as
conference papers, books, reports, and articles published outside this period are excluded.
Considering the fact that those important and foundational studies on this topic published before
2000 have been well reviewed in Glen (2006) and Kavussanos and Visvikis (2006), this paper,
therefore, focuses on the developments in freight rate research after 2000.
To set a clear research boundary, the main emphasis is on quantitative studies that analyse
characteristics of shipping freight rates, examine the impact of macroeconomic and shipping-
related factors on the shipping freight rates, forecast shipping freight rates, or investigate
economic spillovers between the shipping freight market and other markets. Specifically,
studies related to shipping freight rates are excluded if their focus is on other topics. For
example, some studies used freight rate as an independent variable to analyse other markets.
After filtering out the articles that are not peer-reviewed academic papers, the abstracts of the
remaining articles were manually inspected to further filter out the irrelevant papers based on
the inclusion/exclusion criteria, 168 articles were finally selected, and were thoroughly reviewed
and analysed.
2.3. Basic overview and next steps towards in-depth analysis
After collecting the final 168 papers, the review process begins with an overview to obtain some
general and basic observations. The next step is to categorize the studies into a few groups based on
their research objectives and summarize and compare the analysis methods for each research
purpose. The major research findings and progresses are also discussed by research groups and
shipping sectors. Finally, we identify the research trends and gaps. This helps set future research
directions synchronously across the research objectives and shipping sectors.

3. Research objectives and models
This section starts with a categorization of the reviewed papers based on their research objectives,
followed by a discussion of the general choices of methods across research objectives and shipping
sectors.
3.1. Research objectives
The research objectives of the reviewed papers are divided into the following four groups:
(1) analysing time series characteristics of shipping freight rates,
(2) analysing the factors affecting the temporal dynamics of shipping freight rates,
(3) forecasting shipping freight rates, and
(4) analysing the factors affecting the cross-sectional dynamics of shipping freight rates.

The categorization is based on two kinds of dynamics in freight rates: one is temporal dynamics,
containing time-based patterns and trends (related to objective groups (1), (2), and (3)); and the second
is cross-sectional dynamics that describes changes in freight rates caused by external factors irrespective
of time (related to objective groups (4)). Studies on temporal dynamics involve papers that (1) analyse
time series characteristics including stationarity, seasonality, cyclic patterns, etc., usually using uni-
variate models and focusing on the freight rate itself; (2) analyse external factors affecting freight rates
over time; and (3) forecast future freight rates. Studies on cross-sectional dynamics of freight rates are
categorized as objective group (4). This group has similarity to group (2) as both investigate the
influence of external factors on shipping freight rates. They are analysed separately due to the
difference in research methods (i.e., time series analysis and non-time series analysis). Furthermore,
while papers in group (2) often involve macroeconomic factors and study spillover effects between
different markets, papers in group (4) capture the influencing factors at a micro-level such as trading
routes, contract types, and vessel characteristics (Adland, Cariou, and Charles Wolff 2017).
Figure 2 presents numbers of papers in each objective group. Specifically, the topics of the first
group (35 papers) include seasonality, stationarity, or independent and identical distribution of
shipping freight rates. This group also includes papers that attempted to detect bubbles, identify
shipping cycles, and study data volatility clustering characteristics.
The second group (96 papers) attempts to estimate the factors affecting the temporal dynamics of
shipping freight rates or analyse the spillover effects between the shipping freight markets and other
markets, such as the newbuilding market and commodity market, or examine the relationship
between different shipping freight markets.
The third group has 33 papers that aim to forecast freight rates using univariate or multivariate
models.
The fourth group involves 20 papers that investigate the factors affecting the cross-sectional
dynamics of freight rates. Although micro-level fixture data are widely available, only six papers
used individual fixture data to conduct micro-level analysis and evaluate the vessel and contract-
specific factors. This group also includes 12 papers that study trade and port-specific effects on
container freight rates and two papers studying macro-level factors (e.g., ship price and commodity
price) of dry bulk freight rates without considering the time effect.
Clearly, the focus of the existing shipping freight modelling literature is on the temporal
dynamics of the shipping freight rates, in comparison to the cross-sectional dynamics. This
preference of research focus is also directly reflected in data choices. Approximately 93% papers
used highly aggregated data as freight rate measures, that is, either freight rate indices (about 50%)
or quasi-index data (about 43%) that were processed based on relevant market fixtures and
presented the market average for each vessel type and/or route at each time. Furthermore, only

3.2. Quantitative models
3.2.1. Overview of models
A wide variety of quantitative models have been applied to shipping freight rate analysis, as
summarized in Table 2. The mostly used models were econometric methods (75%), including the
relatively new and popular autoregressive conditional heteroskedasticity (ARCH)-type models,
vector autoregressive (VAR), and vector error correction models (VECMs). Besides, equilibrium
model that explain the behaviour of supply, demand, and freight rates and other data-driven
mathematical approaches such as empirical mode decomposition, multifractal detrended fluctua-
tion analysis, etc. that are designed to handle nonstationary and nonlinear data, were also used for
freight rate studies. Moreover, machine learning methods applied to shipping freight rate analysis
were only limited to artificial neural network (ANN) and support vector machine (SVM)
algorithms.
3.2.2. Models by research objectives
Breaking down the applications based on research objectives, approximately one-third papers (54)
delt with the shipping freight rates per se using univariate models, and the other two-thirds (116
papers) analysed factor impacts using multivariate models. There was a sharp contrast between the
bulk sector versus the container sector in terms of univariate model applications (about 64% vs. 9%)
which is mostly due to the data quality varieties in each sector.
The dry bulk sector has the earliest and most varieties of daily indices, which are regularly
published by international shipping organizations, mainly the Baltic Exchange. In contrast, the
container sector had only weekly indices, CCFI and SCFI, both based on containerized export trade
from China. Only after 2020, the daily global container freight index-the Freightos Baltic Index
(FBX) became available (Baltic Exchange 2020). Furthermore, there has been no charter rate index
for the container sector.
The univariate econometric models are either used for time series characteristics analysis or
forecasting. The traditional econometric models applied here are autoregressive moving average
(ARMA), autoregressive integrated moving average (ARIMA), and ARCH-type models. Other
nonlinear and nonparametric methods such as empirical mode decomposition (EMD), multifractal
detrended fluctuation analysis (MF-DFA), rescaled range analysis, stochastic differential equation

Forecasting papers predominantly focused on the freight rate temporal dynamics in the dry bulk

methods, they are normally not suitable for interpreting factor inter-relationships, which is often of
great interest to shipping economists.
For factor analysis of the dynamic or static features, some of the multivariate econometric
models applied include linear time series models focusing on dynamic factor interactions, linear
non-time-series models focusing on static relationships, equilibrium models focusing on economic
interpretation of the market driven factors. These models can be generally compared as data-driven
models versus hypothesis-driven models. The representative models of the former include VAR
models and VECMs which both can be modified to include contemporaneous effects and the latter
is often used in cases of cointegrated variables. However, they do offer theoretical support and
explanation for the choices of variables and lag lengths.
Alternatively, hypothesis-driven models, represented by the SEM and the equilibrium models,
are easier to interpret, but require prior knowledge about the forces influencing variables and prior
differentiation between endogenous and exogenous variables. Thus, they were much less used in
shipping freight rate modelling compared to VAR models and VECMs. Equilibrium theory
provides a good theoretical foundation for regression modelling, for example, 3-stage least squares
(3SLS) estimation (Luo, Fan, and Liu 2009). Applying the equilibrium models to simulation allows
an extensive analysis of freight rate dynamics and explicit scenario testing.
Finally, system dynamics (SD) simulation is suitable for complex dynamics systems that involve
internal feedbacks and time delays. It also allows nonlinear relationships and can integrate stochas-
tic processes to demonstrate the freight market behaviour. However, data requirements for SD
simulations are intensive, restricting its extensive application in modelling shipping freight rates.

shipping freight rates will be discussed in detail.
4. Determination of shipping freight rates
4.1. Disparity across shipping sectors
The three main shipping sectors, that is, dry bulk, tanker, and container, have very different
characteristics and competitive market structures. While the bulk shipping market has been
characterized as perfectly competitive, where similar ships compete for homogeneous cargoes
(Stopford 2009), the container shipping sector is characterized with dynamic market competition
conditions caused by frequent mergers and acquisitions, and an increasing market concentration
level. Within the bulk shipping market, dry and wet (i.e., tanker) bulk markets also differ due to the
very different commodities transported. Stopford (2009) compared dry cargo and tanker freight
rates for the period 1947-2008 and showed that tanker freight rates had higher volatility.
A potential explanation for this observation could be that commodity prices and freight rates are
correlated and prices of liquid bulk such as crude oil are more volatile than prices of dry bulk such
as wheat and steel. Therefore, it is important to investigate the differences among the shipping
sectors and differentiate them while studying the freight rates.

A close inspection of Table 4 reveals that dry bulk and tanker sectors have many more common
factor choices compared to the container sector. The special choices for the container shipping
sector are extrapolated below:
(1) Container sector analyses use the seaborne trade volume and transport distance as the
demand indicators more often, compared to the other two sectors which mostly use
commodity prices for freight rate studies. This is probably because the liner business serves
too diversified cargo types to use a commodity price indicator.
(2) For the container sector, factors representing service efficiency are often considered for
freight rate analysis, as shippers in the container sector are the transport service receivers,
instead of charterers who may manage the transport of cargos by themselves in the dry
bulk and tanker sectors. Examples include service frequency (Márquez-Ramos et al.
2011; Suárez-Alemán, Campos, and Luis Jiménez 2015), connectivity (measured by the
number of services [Márquez-Ramos et al. 2011; Wilmsmeier and Hoffmann 2008] or
connectivity index [De Oliveira 2014]), and port conditions, such as port infrastructure
(De Oliveira 2014; Wilmsmeier and Hoffmann 2008) and port utilization (Lei and
Bachmann 2019).
(3) Another unique factor for container freight rate analysis is trade imbalance (Wilmsmeier
and Hoffmann 2008; Márquez-Ramos et al. 2011; De Oliveira 2014). This is reasonable
because empty container reposition cost caused by trade imbalances can be significant for
the liners. UNCTAD (2015) also reported that higher trade deficits lead to lower export
container freight rates for a country.
(4) Furthermore, market competition has also been included specifically for the container
sector, as the container sector has experienced frequent mergers and acquisitions, establish-
ment, and reconstruction of shipping alliances, and increasing market concentration level.
Several studies have found the market competition in the container shipping sector, mea-
sured by the number of carriers (Suárez-Alemán, Campos, and Luis Jiménez 2015;
Wilmsmeier and Hoffmann 2008) and market concentration index (Hirata 2017; De
Oliveira 2014), has a significantly negative impact on container freight rates.
(5) Finally, two papers included maximum vessel size (Gouvernal and Slack 2012; Wilmsmeier
and Hoffmann 2008) in container freight rate model. Increasingly larger container ships
have been built over the past decades to reduce unit operating cost. To verify this effect
empirically, both papers examined and confirmed an inverse relationship between the
maximum vessel size in the market and freight rate. Different from the container sector,
the studies on bulk freight rates investigated vessel charter prices that allow reference to
vessel sizes explicitly, instead of being limited to the largest ship size in market.
The disparity of the influencing factor choices across the shipping sectors comes from their
different operation characteristics including different cargo forms, transporting, and handling
equipment, and organization approaches. Most dry bulk and tanker carriers carry raw or semi-
raw materials with solid or liquid forms organized based on each individual shipment, while

container shipping carriers generally use containers and complicated liner services to transport
manufactured goods.
Another notable finding from Table 4 is that the tanker sector often uses the number of ships in
the market, under demolition, and new delivery to represent the supply, while dry bulk studies use
a more general supply factor, that is, fleet capacity. This is because of the more designated vessels in
the tanker sector, which makes it possible to focus on a special cargo type and tanker type.
Additionally, macroeconomics indicators (stock and job market indicators) are usually used only
for the dry bulk sector, as major dry bulk commodities are the major raw materials for production
inputs and are commonly accepted as an indicator of the overall macroeconomic developments.
Although the studies appear to agree on the disparity across shipping sectors, the appropriate
influencing factors for each freight rate measurement remains an evolving topic. For example, Bai
and Siu Lee Lam (2017) showed that using shipping demand in ton-miles influences the number of
ships, which further affects freight rates in the very large gas carrier (VLGC) shipping market.
However, Jane Jing, Leung Yip, and Liu (2011) revealed that freight rates Granger cause new-
building prices, and rejected the directional relationship from newbuilding prices to freight rates
using both VECMs and VAR models. Frederik and Nomikos (2019) used AIS data to generate
supply estimates of the fleet supply condition. Using VECM, the authors found that the freight rate
evolution depends on the fleet's geographical distribution and its employment status.
In addition to the general economic factors listed in Table 4, the interactions of different types of
freight rates were studied. For example, 27 papers analysed the interactions of spot rates with FFA
rates, three papers on spot rates and TC rates, and five papers on across market sectors. A general
conclusion has been made that FFA rates contribute to the discovery of new information in the spot

4.2. Influence of vessel characteristics and contract terms
In addition to the factors listed in Table 4, it is worth noting that a limited number (seven) of papers
have investigated vessel specifications and contract details' influence on freight rates. Specifically,
Adland, Cariou, and Charles Wolff (2017) found that around 70-80% of rate variation is explained
by the time fixed effects while the remainder is due to vessel characteristics, contractual terms, and
the influence of charterers in an analysis of the Offshore Support Vessel market.
Alizadeh and Talley (2011; 2011b) investigated the influence of the laycan period, the vessel's
hull type (for tanker), deadweight, vessel age, and voyage routes. Roar and Alizadeh (2018)
considered vessel specifications and contractual terms, and Adland, Cariou, and Charles Wolff
(2016) and Adland, Cariou, and Charles Wolff (2017) focused on the influence of the characteristics
of charterers and ship owners. Another two papers that worth notice are Köhn and Thanopoulou
(2011) and Agnolucci, Smith, and Rehmatulla (2014) which specifically considered fuel efficiency
effect, in addition to vessel and charter term characteristics. The latter found that fuel efficiency
leads to a freight rate premium for the Panamax dry bulk TC market during 2008-2012.
Furthermore, ship owners recoup approximately 40% of the fuel savings through higher rates.
5. Time series characteristics of shipping freight rates
5.1. Stationarity
Despite the long time and intensive research focus on time series analysis of shipping freight rates, it
is still debatable whether freight rates are truly non-stationary. Tvedt (2003), Adland and Cullinane
(2006), Koekebakker, Adland, and Sødal (2006), Kou, Luo, and Zhao (2018), and Población (2017)
contributed in-depth analyses and discussions about the stationary of bulk freight rates in levels.
They all mentioned that the equilibrium freight rate resulting from the interaction between supply
and demand should be mean reverting (stationary), as the bulk shipping freight market is a (close
to) perfectly competitive market (Stopford 2009).
Specifically, Tvedt (2003) found evidence on the stationary nature of freight rates (weekly dry
bulk spot rate and BFI) by changing the perspective from US$ to Japanese yen. Koekebakker,
Adland, and Sødal (2006)'s research results of weekly spot and forward freight rates of five tanker
routes are also in line with this theory in a non-linear setting. Adland and Cullinane (2006)'s
empirical findings suggested that the spot rate in the tanker sector is locally non-stationary over the
range of the process. However, the existence of a non-linear mean-reverting drift at the edges of the
range is sufficient to pull the series back into its middle region and determine the global stationarity.
Kou, Luo, and Zhao (2018) drew the same conclusion from observations in the dry bulk sector that
long-term freight rate samples (between 26 to 42 years) are stationary. However, merely increasing
the sample length would not make the freight rates stationary, while sample windows separated by
structural breaks are tested as stationary.
Yet, with only a few exceptions (e.g., SCFI monthly freight indexes on different routes in Jiang,
Jian, and Gong 2018), most reviewed articles have treated shipping freight rates in levels as non-
stationary based on common stationarity tests like Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF), Phillips-
Perron (PP), and Kwiatkowski-Phillips-Schmidt-Shin (KPSS) tests. Therefore, most papers have
taken the first differences, or the first logarithmic differences of shipping freight rates. However, this
may have been a problematic practice considering that over-differencing may cause many issues in

5.2. Seasonality and cycles
A few papers have systematically investigated the seasonal behaviour of freight rates. Kavussanos
and Alizadeh-M (2001, 2002) and Yin and Shi (2018) found deterministic seasonality in the dry
bulk, tanker, and container sectors, respectively, while Poblacion (2015) concluded that seasonality
in bulk freight rates is stochastic. Población and Serna (2018) found that the seasonal period is
approximately one year in the tanker market.
There are ten papers analysing the shipping cycles. Using the same freight rate data and
applying rescaled range analysis with V-statistic, Goulielmos (2010) detected medium cycles of 10
and 20 years. This result is in line with Stopford (2009), who identified 22 dry cargo freight cycles
between 1741 and 2007. Using the same method as Goulielmos (2010) and more recent data (i.e.,
1-year time charter rate from 1989 to 2008), Goulielmos and Elpiniki Psifia (2011) found a short
term 4-year cycle. Chen et al. (2019) found the short-term cycle of the market is 41.2 months (i.e.,
3.43 years), the medium-to-long-term cycles are 7.11 years to 8.97 years, and the long-term cycle
is 11.96 years for the Aframax tanker market using wavelet analysis. Chistè and van Vuuren
(2014) extracted a longer 7-year cycle and a shorter 4-year cycle from the BDI using Fourier
analysis. Moreover, Yijie, Yin, and Sheng (2018) found that the shipping cycles have impacts on
the causality relationship among the freight rate, second-hand price, and new-build ship price in
the dry bulk shipping market. The causality could only be identified during the trough and peak
periods.
5.3. Volatility clustering
Volatility clustering is another important characteristic of the time series of shipping freight rates.
ARCH-type models are often used as they can capture the heteroskedasticity of many freight rate
data and examine how variables influence the mean and variance of the shipping freight rates.
However, Engelen et al. (2011) argued that nonlinearities, the rejection of normality, and excess
kurtosis and skewness indicate the misleading nature of ARCH-type models with respect to
stationarity. Therefore, many researchers chose nonparametric specifications (e.g., rescaled range
analysis and MF-DFA) that allow one to determine the correlation properties of both non-
stationary and stationary time series. Specific examples are Chen et al. (2016) and Tingyi et al.
(2018) that used MF-DFA to study the volatility of dry bulk and tanker freight rates, respectively;
Liu and Chen (2018) applied MF-DFA to investigate the asymmetric volatility of the dry bulk
shipping market under structural breaks. However, these nonparametric approaches in the litera-
ture were mostly limited to univariate analysis, while multivariate GARCH models were also used to
investigate the volatility spillover effects among different markets.

7. Conclusion
This review provides a comprehensive discussion of shipping freight rate research. In addition to
exploring developments and limitations in the existing literature for the interest of shipping
economists, it also intends to help shipping practitioners better understand the shipping freight
market. For shipping speculators and investors interested in future freight rates developments, this
paper compares different prediction models with different freight rate data and the main findings.
For carriers and other shipping operators who are interested in maximizing profitability or risk
control, this review provides a clear roadmap to the related research and thoroughly discusses the
influencing factors that have been analysed in literature.

7.1. Trends and achievements
Only 22 papers (13.1% of the shipping freight rate papers) in the past 20 years studied container
freight rates, 21 of which investigated the container liner shipping freight rates charged from
shippers and the remaining one studied containership charter rates and the correlations among
different shipping sectors.
In terms of research objectives, more focus was given to temporal dynamics compared to cross-
sectional dynamics caused by micro-level factors (e.g., vessel characteristics, contract terms).
However, even though intensively discussed, some topics regarding the temporal dynamic char-
acteristics remain controversial, such as the stationarity issue.
In terms of model choices, a wide range of modelling methods are used. Overall, classic
econometrics remains the most popular method, while machine learning algorithms only
increased slightly since they first appeared in the shipping literature ten years ago. However,
a general finding is that machine learning outperforms classic econometric methods in forecast-
ing. Innovative hybrid models constructed by a few papers were also successful compared with
the original models.
In terms of influencing factors, studies for the three shipping sectors have presented quite
different findings, which accurately reflect the differences in the business operating models and
cargo characteristics across sectors. In other words, the existing literature has reflected the well-
established and consistent understanding of the shipping sector operating characteristics.
A further analysis of the authorship networks indicates that this is a very concentrated network
with several major networks making significant contributions. Approximately 71% authors
published only one paper on this topic. And six major institutions contributed to this topic
with 27% of the authorship. There is a lack of cross-group, cross-field, or cross-institutional
collaborations.
7.2. Gaps and future directions
First, compared to temporal analysis, cross-sectional analysis that could reveal the freight rate
mechanism (e.g., explaining influences of operational factors on freight rates) is highly under-
developed. These analyses could provide useful guidance to industrial practitioners for making
better operational decisions. As discussed in Section 4.2, the existing seven papers studying the
influences of vessel characteristics and contract terms on freight rates have revealed important
factor relationships, however, did not cover the entire shipping market. The cross-sectional analysis
using fixture data can be extended to, for example, containership time charter rate, the Supramax
market and the liquefied natural gas (LNG) market. In the container sector specifically, the impact
of non-charter related cross-sectional factors such as port congestion, empty container availability
and US dollar exchange rate on freight rates are considered in shipping practices but not yet
investigated extensively in the scientific literature.
Second, as discussed earlier, when investigating the container freight rates, the focus was
predominantly on shipping freight rates charged from shippers, while the containership charter
rates as a major cost for the carriers remain largely overlooked. A better understanding of contain-
ership charter rates and the influencing factors is crucial for carriers to minimize cost and ship-
owners to increase their revenue and capability in speculation.
Third, the (non)stationarity property of the shipping freight rates remains a controversial
topic. The widely used log return methods are useful for shipping investors, but not for other
shipping practitioners who are interested in the freight rate levels. Besides, over-differencing also
causes issues in parameter estimation, for example, biased estimates of the effects of long-
memory processes. With more high-quality data becoming foreseeably available, machine
learning approach is anticipated to be increasingly applied due to its strong predictive power
that is not limited by data stationarity. Additionally, despite its weaknesses in parameter

estimation and interpretability, theory about causal inference in machine learning is available
(e.g., Pearl 2019).
Finally, current collaboration patterns indicate that knowledge sharing, and across-field colla-
boration efforts can be further improved, which will bring more inspired insights and advanced new
methods to this topic. For example, a collaboration between shipping economists and computer
scientists could help expedite the application of state-of-the-art machine learning algorithms in
shipping freight rate studies and might enable the investigation of less-explored cross-sectional
dynamics of shipping freight rates with increased interpretability.
